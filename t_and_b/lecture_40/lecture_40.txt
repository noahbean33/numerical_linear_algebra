Lecture 40: Preconditioning
Preconditioning is a simple, general idea that can require a lot of explanation in practice. Since the convergence of a Krylov method depends (at least) on the spectral properties of the matrix, we can improve the convergence by transforming to an equivalent problem with a different matrix. Specifically,  Ax=b
  becomes  M−1Ax=M−1b
  for some matrix  M
 . We don't typically plug in  M−1
 ; rather we have to solve a system  Mv=u
  whenever we would want  M−1u
 . In essence,  M
  should be an "easily inverted" approximation of  A
 .

We'll experiment with the 5-point difference representation of the Laplacian operator on a square.

del2 = @(n) n^2*delsq( numgrid('S',n) );
spy(del2(10));

As is usually the case in PDEs, the conditioning of  A
  gets worse as the discretization gets better. (After all, the eigenvalues of the exact operator are unbounded.)

for n = 4:4:32
  lambda = eig( full(del2(n)) );
  loglog(n,lambda,'bx'),  hold on
end
plot(4:32,8*(4:32).^2,'r--')
text(4,130,'O(n^2)','color','red','horiz','r')
title('Conditioning growth')
xlabel('n')
ylabel('eigenvalues')
axis tight

Here's what we get applying CG to this matrix at  n=160
 . (We're plotting the solution of the Poisson equation,  Δu=−1
 .)

n = 160;
A = del2(n);  b = ones(length(A),1);  
[x,flag,rr,iter,rvec] = pcg(A,b,1e-8,250);
surf( reshape(x,n-2,n-2) )
title('solution')
shading flat

semilogy(0:length(rvec)-1,rvec)
xlabel('Krylov dimension'), ylabel('residual norm')
title('CG convergence (n=160)')

A simple preconditioner might be to strip away the nonzeros of  A
  that are wider than the tridiagonal strip. This  M
  is easier to invert/solve than the original  A
 .

M = spdiags( spdiags(A,-1:1), -1:1, length(A), length(A));
[x,flag,rr,iter,rvecT] = pcg(A,b,1e-8,250,M);

semilogy(0:length(rvec)-1,rvec,'k')
hold on
semilogy(0:length(rvecT)-1,rvecT)
legend('no precond','tridiag')
xlabel('Krylov dimension'), ylabel('residual norm')
title('CG convergence (n=160)')

A more sophisticated strategy is the incomplete Cholesky factorization. There are various forms; here, we compute a Cholesky factor but zero out any elements that are zero in the same location in  A
 .

R = ichol(A);
fprintf(' %i nonzeros in A; %i nonzeros in M\n\n',nnz(A),nnz(R))
124188 nonzeros in A; 74576 nonzeros in M
[x,flag,rr,iter,rvecI] = pcg(A,b,1e-8,250,R',R);
semilogy(0:length(rvec)-1,rvec,'k')
hold on
semilogy(0:length(rvecT)-1,rvecT)
semilogy(0:length(rvecI)-1,rvecI)
legend('no precond','tridiag','cholinc')
xlabel('Krylov dimension'), ylabel('residual norm')
title('CG convergence (n=160)')

In a sense this looks successful, but in fact the number of iterations would still grow as a function of  n
 .

for n = 100:50:300
    A = del2(n);  b = ones(length(A),1);
    R = ichol(A);
    [x,flag,rr,iter,rvec] = pcg(A,b,1e-8,250,R',R);
    semilogy(0:length(rvec)-1,rvec)
    hold on
end
xlabel('Krylov dimension'), ylabel('residual norm')
title('CG/IC for linearly increasing n')
ylim([1e-6 1e4])

In scientific computation a good preconditioner would keep the number of iterations well under 50 for large  n
 . This usually requires detailed understanding and exploitation of the underlying problem, unlike the purely algebraic approach taken here.
