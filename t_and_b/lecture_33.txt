Lecture 33: Arnoldi iteration¶
Suppose  A∈Cm×m
  and  b∈Cm
  are given. The  n
 th Krylov subspace  Kn
  is the range of a certain  m×n
  matrix:

A = randn(500,500);
b = ones(500,1);
K = b;
n = 10;
for j = 1:n-1
    K = [K A*K(:,j)];
end
One way to think of  K
  is as keeping a history of all the vectors found in the power iteration. For that reason, we suspect that it may be rich in content with the leading eigenvectors of  A
 , much as with simultaneous iteration. However, it's a poorly conditioned basis as  n
  grows, since the columns all become parallel to the leading eigenvector.

cond(K)
ans =

   1.3490e+12
We'd like a (thin) QR factorization of  K
 , of course, but once  K
  has been put into floating point, the information about successive dimensions could be drawn out only through subtractive cancellation. Instead, there is a clever way to arrange the Gram-Schmidt process for this particular matrix.

Say  q1,…,qj
  is an ONB for  Kj
 . By construction,  Aqj∈Kj+1
 . Thus if  qj+1
  extends the ONB to  Kj+1
 , it must be that

Aqj=h1jq1+⋯+hnjqn+hj+1,jqj+1.
 
Furthermore,  hij=q∗iAqj
 . Written out for all  j=1,…,n
 , this implies

AQn=Qn+1H~n,
 
where  Qj
  collects  q1,…,qj
  and  H~n
  is an  (n+1)×n
  upper Hessenberg matrix. Adapting the modified Gram-Schmidt algorithm, where the "new column" of a matrix comes from  A
  times the most recent column, we get the Arnoldi iteration.

m = size(A,1);  n = 50;
Q= zeros(m,n+1);
H = zeros(n+1,n);
Q(:,1) = b/norm(b);
for j = 1:n
    v = A*Q(:,j);
    for i = 1:j
        H(i,j) = dot(Q(:,i),v);
        v = v - H(i,j)*Q(:,i);
    end
    H(j+1,j) = norm(v);
    Q(:,j+1) = v/H(j+1,j);
end
spy(H);

It's not hard to show that we can project  A
  into  Kn
  (in the  Qn
 -basis) by

Q∗nAQn=Q∗nQn+1H~n=Hn,
 
where  Hn
  is  H~n
  with the last row deleted. Eigenvalues of  Hn
  are eigenvalues of the action of  A
  as projected to  Kn
 . As  n→∞
 , this begins to reveal some of the eigenvalues of  A
  itself.

lam = eig(A);
plot(real(lam),imag(lam),'.')
axis square
axis equal
hold on
for j = 5:n
    lam = eig(H(1:j,1:j));
    plot(real(lam),imag(lam),'rx')
end

We'll look more closely at the convergence in the next lecture. For now, the big idea is to replace a problem on a very high-dimensional space,  Cm
 , with an analog on a low-dimensional space,  Kn
 , using a ON basis as provided by the Arnoldi iteration.
