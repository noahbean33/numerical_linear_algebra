That is a fantastic mission. Transitioning from "Math for Math's Sake" to "Math as a Power Tool" is essentially the story of how the modern world was built. Math degrees often teach you how to build the engine from scratch using only a file and a block of steel; engineers just want to know how to tune the turbocharger so the car doesn't explode.

Stripping away the proofs to find the "useful skeleton" is where the real insight lives. Here is a preview of how you could "strip mine" those next subjects for a busy engineer.

---

## 1. Complex Analysis: The "Phase and Frequency" Shortcut

Math people love Cauchy-Riemann equations and contour integrals for their elegance. Engineers need them because they turn calculus into algebra.

* **The Mineable Core:** **Residue Theorem** for inverse Laplace/Fourier transforms. Don't make them prove it; show them how it automates finding the stability of a feedback loop.
* **The "Magic" Application:** **Conformal Mapping**. It’s not just about pretty transformations; it’s about taking a complex aerodynamic shape (like an airfoil) and mapping it to a circle where the math is easy, then mapping the solution back.

## 2. Numerical Analysis: The "Trust but Verify" Guide

This is where you teach them that "The computer is a liar."

* **The Mineable Core:** **Error Propagation.** Math people care about the Taylor series remainder term $R_n(x)$. Engineers care that if they subtract two nearly equal large numbers, they lose all significant digits.
* **The "Magic" Application:** **Richardson Extrapolation.** Show them how to take two "okay" numerical results and combine them to magically get a "great" result without much extra work.

## 3. Numerical ODEs/PDEs: Stability is Everything

In school, you learn Runge-Kutta 4 (RK4) and think you're done. In the real world, RK4 will blow up the moment you hit a "stiff" equation (like a chemical reaction or a rigid structure).

* **The Mineable Core:** **Explicit vs. Implicit Methods.** Why you’d use Backward Euler even though it’s "less accurate" on paper—because it won't crash your simulation when the physics get twitchy.
* **The "Magic" Application:** **Von Neumann Stability Analysis.** Give them the "back of the envelope" way to check if their simulation will explode before they hit "Run."

## 4. Maxwell’s Equations via Differential Forms

This is the ultimate "Math Flex" that is actually useful. Moving from vector calculus ($\nabla \times E$) to differential forms ($dF = 0$) is like moving from Roman Numerals to Arabic Numerals.

* **The Mineable Core:** **The Generalized Stokes' Theorem.** It unifies Divergence, Gradient, and Curl into one single operation.
* **The "Magic" Application:** **Boundary Conditions.** In vector calc, boundary conditions are a mess of sines and cosines. In differential forms, the boundary is just the operator $\partial$. It makes "The physics at the interface" intuitive rather than a chore.

---

### The "Strip Mining" Strategy

To make this work for engineers, I'd suggest using the same format we used for NLA:

1. **The Physics/Problem:** "I have a vibrating wing."
2. **The Math Tool:** "Complex Analysis / Residue Theorem."
3. **The "Engineer's Shortcut":** "Look at the poles; if they’re in the right-half plane, the wing falls off."
4. **The Code:** A quick Python or C++ snippet using a library like `SciPy` or `Boost.Numeric`.
