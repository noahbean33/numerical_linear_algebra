**Computational cost and memory layout first, proofs second.** ECE students need to internalize that an algorithm's flop count and memory access pattern matter more than its elegance. Start with how matrices actually live in memory (row-major vs column-major, stride, cache lines) before touching any factorization. This is the bridge between their hardware intuition and the math.

**Core factorizations with hardware motivation.** LU, Cholesky, QR, and SVD — but always tied to *why* an engineer would reach for each one. LU for solving linear systems in circuit simulation (SPICE does this thousands of times). Cholesky for symmetric positive definite systems that show up everywhere in estimation and optimization. QR for least squares in signal processing. SVD for dimensionality reduction, noise filtering, and understanding ill-conditioning.

**Sparse matrices and iterative methods.** This is where ECE diverges hardest from a generic numerical linear algebra course. Finite element meshes, circuit netlists, and graph Laplacians all produce massive sparse systems. Conjugate gradient, GMRES, and preconditioning are more practically important to ECE people than dense eigenvalue algorithms. Emphasize sparsity structure exploitation — banded, block-diagonal, etc.

**Fixed-point and finite precision arithmetic.** Most numerical LA courses treat floating point as a given and do rounding error analysis in IEEE 754. For ECE students, go further: what happens when you're implementing a matrix solve on an FPGA or DSP with 16-bit fixed-point? How does quantization error propagate through a factorization? Condition numbers stop being abstract and become design constraints.

**FFT as a linear algebra operation.** ECE students already know the FFT from signals, but most don't realize it's a sparse matrix factorization of the DFT matrix. Connecting the Cooley-Tukey butterfly to sparse matrix-vector products is a powerful unifying insight. It also opens the door to discussing structured matrices (Toeplitz, circulant) and how convolution becomes multiplication in the frequency domain — which is really just a diagonalization.

**Eigenvalue problems with physical grounding.** Modal analysis of structures, stability analysis of control systems, principal component analysis in sensor arrays. The power method and QR algorithm matter, but motivate them through applications they'll actually encounter. For control people, Schur decomposition and matrix exponentials. For signal processing people, eigendecomposition of covariance matrices.

**Numerical stability as an engineering constraint.** Rather than doing the full Wilkinson-style backward error analysis, I'd focus on building intuition: why does Gaussian elimination without pivoting blow up, why is the normal equations approach to least squares dangerous for ill-conditioned problems, and how do you diagnose and fix numerical issues in practice. Condition numbers as the "gain" of error amplification — that analog maps directly to their circuit intuition.

**Parallel and hardware-aware computation.** BLAS levels (1, 2, 3) and why Level-3 operations are fast. How matrix tiling maps to cache hierarchy. GPU-friendly formulations. This connects directly to their understanding of memory bandwidth, latency, and throughput from computer architecture courses.

The meta-principle: ECE students think in terms of systems, signals, and hardware constraints. Every algorithm should be presented with its computational complexity, memory footprint, numerical stability profile, and at least one concrete engineering application. Skip the measure-theoretic motivation, lean into the "what breaks and when" intuition.
