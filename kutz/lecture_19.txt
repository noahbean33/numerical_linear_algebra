## **Lecture Notes: Implementing Randomized Linear Algebra (RLA)**

These notes cover the implementation and practical application of randomized algorithms for computing the **Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)**. This approach is particularly powerful for massive data matrices that are too large to fit in memory.

---

### **1. Core Concept: Low-Rank Structure**

The fundamental assumption behind randomized linear algebra is that the data matrix $A$ possesses a **low-rank structure**. Even if a matrix has millions of columns, its essential patterns (column space) can often be spanned by a much smaller representative subspace.

* 
**Objective**: Sample the column space of a large matrix $A$ using random projections to find these dominant patterns.


* 
**The Power of Randomness**: In high-dimensional spaces, two random vectors are orthogonal with very high probability. This allows us to sample the column space in diverse "orthogonal directions" effectively.



---

### **2. The Two-Stage Algorithm**

The process of implementing a randomized SVD is typically broken into two stages.

#### **Stage A: Finding the Orthonormal Basis ($Q$)**

The goal of this stage is to find a matrix $Q$ whose columns form an orthonormal basis for the column space of $A$.

1. 
**Generate a Random Sampling Matrix ($\Omega$)**: Create an $n \times k$ matrix of random numbers (typically Gaussian distributed).


2. 
**Form a Proxy Matrix ($Y$)**: Compute $Y = A\Omega$. This matrix $Y$ (size $m \times k$) is a linear combination of the columns of $A$ in a random order.


3. 
**Compute the QR Decomposition**: Perform a QR decomposition on $Y$ to obtain $Q$.


* 
**Result**: $Q$ is an $m \times k$ matrix with $k$ orthonormal columns that span the column space of $A$.





#### **Stage B: Projection and SVD**

Once the subspace $Q$ is defined, the SVD is computed on a much smaller, more manageable matrix.

1. 
**Project the Data**: Project the original matrix $A$ into the subspace defined by $Q$ by computing $B = Q^T A$.


2. 
**Compute SVD of $B$**: Perform the SVD on the smaller matrix $B$ (size $k \times n$) to find $\tilde{U}, \tilde{\Sigma}, \text{and } \tilde{V}$.


3. 
**Project Back**: To find the approximation of the original left-singular vectors ($U$), multiply $Q$ by the computed $\tilde{U}$: $U \approx Q\tilde{U}$.



---

### **3. Choosing the Sampling Parameter ($k$)**

The number of random projections, $k$, is the "magic number" in this algorithm.

* 
**Ideal Choice**: $k$ should be the target rank ($r$) plus a small oversampling buffer ($l$), i.e., $k = r + l$.


* **Performance Trade-offs**:
* 
**Low $k$ (e.g., $k=5$)**: Can result in high variability and poor approximations of the true singular modes.


* 
**Higher $k$ (e.g., $k=15$)**: Generally provides a much more stable and accurate reconstruction of the dominant modes.

### **4. Case Study: Soliton Evolution**

The lecture demonstrates the algorithm using synthetic data from the **Nonlinear Schrödinger Equation**, which simulates "soliton" pulses over time.

| Metric | Observation |
| --- | --- |
| **Matrix Size** | <br>$1024 \times 41$ (Small enough for ground truth comparison).

 |
| **Variance Captured** | The first 3 modes captured approximately **99%** of the variance.

 |
| **Low-Rank Interpretation** | Dominant patterns are smooth pulses; numerical "tails" drop to $10^{-14}$, indicating redundant information.

 |
| **Accuracy** | With $k=10$ or $15$, the randomized SVD modes were nearly identical to the "ground truth" full SVD modes.

 |

---

### **Summary of Steps**

> 1. Randomly sample the column space.
> 2. Perform QR to get an orthonormal coordinate system.
> 3. Project the large data into this smaller coordinate system.
> 4. Perform SVD on the small matrix and project results back to the original dimension.
