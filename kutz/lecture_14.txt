## Lecture Notes: Modern Algorithms for Computing Eigenvalues

This lecture outlines the generic architectures and modern strategies used by software packages (like Python and MATLAB) to compute eigenvalues and eigenvectors. While the underlying math is sophisticated, most modern algorithms follow a consistent two-step structural framework.

---

### I. The Two-Step Generic Architecture

Instead of searching for eigenvalues directly from a raw matrix $A$, modern solvers utilize a structured, recursive approach:

1. 
**Step 1: Factorization/Reduction:** Perform a constructive, finite-time algorithm to transform the matrix into a simpler, "self-similar" form (e.g., Hessenberg or Tri-diagonal).


2. 
**Step 2: Iteration:** Use an iterative procedure on the simplified matrix to converge toward the final eigenvalues and eigenvectors.



> 
> **Key Concept: Self-Similarity** > Two matrices are self-similar if they share the same eigenvalues. By using unitary transformations, we can simplify the matrix's structure without changing its core characteristic polynomial.
> 
> 

---

### II. Step 1: Factorization and Form Reduction

The goal of the first step is to zero out specific elements of the matrix to make iteration computationally "cheaper".

#### 1. Schur Factorization

This is a principal method for decomposing a matrix $A$ into a unitary matrix $Q$ and an upper triangular matrix $T$:


$$A = QTQ^*$$

* 
**$Q$** is a unitary transformation (meaning its inverse is its adjoint, $Q^*$).


* 
**$T$** is upper triangular.


* If the original matrix $A$ is **Hermitian** (symmetric), $T$ must also be Hermitian; because $T$ is upper triangular, this forces it to be **diagonal**.



#### 2. Hessenberg and Tri-diagonal Forms

* 
**Generic Matrices:** Reduced to **Upper Hessenberg Form**, where all elements below the first sub-diagonal are zeroed out.


* 
**Hermitian Matrices:** Reduced to **Tri-diagonal Form** (only the main diagonal and the diagonals immediately above and below it contain non-zero values).



---

### III. Step 2: Iteration to Conclusion

Once the matrix is in a simplified form (like Hessenberg), the algorithm enters a loop to find the actual values.

* 
**Historical Standard (1960s–1990s):** The **QR Algorithm** was the dominant iterative scheme due to its fast convergence.


* 
**Modern Standard (1990s–Present):** **Divide and Conquer** algorithms are now considered state-of-the-art. They work by partitioning the complex plane and looking for eigenvalues in specific regions.


* 
**Note:** While simple "power iterations" are useful for finding the largest eigenvalue, they are rarely used in professional-grade solvers today.



---

### IV. Case Study: Computing the SVD

The Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is inherently an eigenvalue problem. It relies on the fact that $A^*A$ and $AA^*$ are always Hermitian, making their eigenvalues easier to compute.

#### The Golub-Kahan Algorithm

Pioneered in the 1960s, this algorithm transformed scientific computing by allowing stable SVD calculations for large matrices. It follows the two-step process:

1. 
**Bi-diagonalization:** Using two different bases ($U$ and $V$), the matrix $A$ is reduced to a form with values only on the main diagonal and one upper diagonal.


2. 
**Diagonalization:** The bi-diagonal matrix is iterated (originally via QR, now via Divide and Conquer) until only the singular values remain on the diagonal.
