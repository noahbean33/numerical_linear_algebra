## Lecture Notes: Applied Linear Algebra — GMRES

These notes cover the transition from direct solvers to iterative methods, specifically focusing on the **Generalized Method of Residuals (GMRES)** and the underlying **Krylov Subspace** theory.

---

### 1. The Motivation for Iterative Methods

While direct methods like LU decomposition, Gaussian elimination, QR, or SVD are robust, they face a significant computational bottleneck as problem sizes increase.

* 
**The $O(n^3)$ Problem**: Most direct solvers for square matrices require approximately $n^3$ operations.


* 
**Hardware vs. Algorithm Progress**: Between 1950 and 1995, hardware capabilities increased by a factor of $10^9$, yet the size of matrices we could solve only increased by $10^3$. This disparity exists because to solve a matrix ten times larger, you need a thousand times more computing power.


* 
**The Goal**: Iterative methods aim to circumvent this $n^3$ bottleneck, allowing us to solve massive systems (billions of variables) used in modern fluid flow or galaxy simulations.



---

### 2. Krylov Subspaces

The foundation of modern iterative solvers is the **Krylov Subspace**. Given a matrix $A$ and a vector $b$, the Krylov subspace $\mathcal{K}_n$ is the span of the vectors generated by repeatedly applying $A$ to $b$.

**Definition**:


$$\mathcal{K}_n(A, b) = \text{span} \{b, Ab, A^2b, \dots, A^{n-1}b\}$$



* 
**Orthonormal Basis**: Because the vectors in the Krylov matrix $K_n$ are not necessarily orthogonal, we use a **QR decomposition** to find an orthonormal basis $Q_n$ for the subspace.


* 
**Gram-Schmidt Process**: The basis is built recursively: $q_1$ is the normalized $b$, and each subsequent $q_n$ is found by projecting out previous directions and normalizing.



---

### 3. The GMRES Algorithm

The **Generalized Method of Residuals (GMRES)** exploits the Krylov subspace to find an approximate solution $x_n$ to the system $Ax = b$.

#### The Core Concept

At each iteration $n$, GMRES seeks a vector $x_n$ within the Krylov subspace that **minimizes the residual** ($r_n$).


$$r_n = \|b - Ax_n\|_2$$



* 
**Representation**: We represent the approximate solution as $x_n = Q_n y$, where $Q_n$ is the orthonormal basis and $y$ is a vector of loadings to be determined.


* 
**Optimization**: The problem becomes a **least-squares fit** to find the $y$ that minimizes the residual in the transformed coordinate system.



---

### 4. Arnoldi Iterations

To perform GMRES efficiently, we use the **Arnoldi Iteration**, which connects the Krylov subspace to a **Hessenberg matrix** ($H$).

* 
**Self-Similarity**: We transform $A$ into a Hessenberg form using a unitary matrix $Q$.


* 
**Recursion Formula**: Arnoldi provides a way to find $q_{n+1}$ using only the previous $q$ vectors and the matrix $A$.


* **The Relation**:


$$A Q_n = Q_{n+1} \tilde{H}_n$$


This relationship allows us to solve the minimization problem in a lower-dimensional space ($n \times n$) rather than the original massive $m \times m$ space.



---

### 5. Summary of the Iterative Procedure

1. 
**Initialize**: Start with $q_1 = b / \|b\|$.


2. **Iterate**:
* Use the **Arnoldi recursion** to find the next orthonormal vector $q_{n+1}$.


* Solve the **least-squares problem** to find $y$ that minimizes the residual $\|H_n y - \|b\|e_1\|$.


* Update the solution $x_n = Q_n y$.




3. 
**Converge**: Repeat until the residual is sufficiently small.



**Matlab Implementation**:

```matlab
% Solving Ax = b using GMRES
x = gmres(A, b);

```
